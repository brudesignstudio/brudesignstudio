Why do websites cost so much?

By Bru Design Studio·7 min read

You asked for a website quote and nearly fell out of your chair. You're not alone. The numbers can range from a few hundred dollars to tens of thousands, and nobody seems to give a straight answer about why.

So let's break it down honestly.

The real reasons websites are expensive

1. You're not just paying for a pretty page

A website is not a flyer. It's a living, functional piece of software that needs to load fast, work on every device, rank on Google, connect to payment systems, capture leads, and represent your brand without fail, 24 hours a day. When you hire a designer or agency, you're paying for:

  • Strategy — figuring out what the site actually needs to do for your business.
  • Design — creating a visual experience that builds trust and converts visitors.
  • Development — writing or configuring the code that makes everything work.
  • Copywriting — the words on your site, which most people underestimate.
  • Testing — making sure nothing breaks across browsers, screen sizes, and devices.
  • Launch and setup — domains, hosting, SSL certificates, analytics, and more.

Each of those is a skill. Most agencies have separate people handling each one. You're paying for the full team, even if you only see one face.

2. Agencies have overhead

Large and mid-size agencies carry serious overhead. Office space, project managers, account managers, sales teams, software subscriptions, insurance, and payroll all get baked into your quote before a single pixel is designed. When an agency charges $15,000 for a website, a significant portion isn't going toward your project. It's keeping the lights on. That's not a criticism, it's just math. But it does explain why the final number can feel disconnected from the actual work being done.

3. Custom work takes time

A fully custom website, built from scratch with unique layouts, animations, and functionality, can take weeks or months. Senior developers bill anywhere from $75 to $200 per hour, and designers are not far behind. Add up the real hours in discovery calls, revisions, development, and feedback cycles, and a $10,000 price tag can actually represent a lean project.

The problem is that most small businesses don't need fully custom builds. They need something clean, fast, professional, and effective. But agencies price everything like it's a custom project because that's how they protect their margins.

4. Templates are not always the answer either

You might be thinking, "What about Squarespace or Wix? Isn't that cheap?" Yes and no. DIY website builders are affordable upfront, but they come with real costs:

  • Time — learning any platform, even a simple one, takes hours you probably don't have.
  • Limitations — template sites often cap what you can do with SEO, speed, and design.
  • Generic results — if everyone can use the same template, your site looks like everyone else's.
  • Hidden fees — e-commerce plans, custom domains, and third-party integrations add up fast.

A DIY site also puts the maintenance burden entirely on you. Every update, plugin conflict, and broken layout becomes your problem.

5. SEO and performance are not optional

A site that looks good but doesn't load fast or rank on Google is a liability, not an asset. Search engine optimization is a technical discipline that requires intentional decisions during the build, not an afterthought you bolt on later. Page speed, clean code, proper heading structure, image compression, mobile responsiveness, and metadata all factor into how Google evaluates your site. Getting these wrong means you're paying for a website that nobody finds.

6. Ongoing costs are real

The price you pay to build a website is not the last check you write. Ongoing costs include hosting ($10 to $100+ per month), domain renewal ($15 to $20 per year), SSL certificates, plugin or theme licenses, security monitoring, and content updates. Some agencies lock you into monthly retainers for these services. Others hand you the keys and disappear. Neither extreme serves small business owners well.

So what should you actually pay?

Here's the honest answer: it depends on what your business needs, not on what sounds impressive. A five-page website for a local service business does not need a $25,000 build. A growing e-commerce brand does not need a $500 template site that will fall apart under volume.

The right price is the one that matches the scope of your actual goals. The problem is that most people shopping for websites don't know enough to evaluate whether a quote is fair, and some agencies take advantage of that. What you should be asking is not just "how much does it cost?" but also:

  • How long will it take?
  • What happens after launch?
  • Will I be able to make basic updates myself?
  • Is this built to grow with my business?
  • Who do I call if something breaks?
If an agency can't answer those questions clearly and quickly, that's a red flag worth paying attention to.
